CHAPTER 1: INTRODUCTION
1.1 BACKGROUND
Malaysia is one of the fastest growing countries in Southeast Asia, with the urban population share rising from approximately 34% in 1980 to more than 77% by 2020 [1]. As a result of this transformation, tropical forests, farmlands, and secondary vegetation have been replaced with roads, commercial buildings, and industrial estates, altering the land’s surface energy balance fundamentally. The majority of solar energy absorbed by natural vegetated surfaces dissipates as latent heat, through evaporation. However, impervious urban materials like asphalt, concrete, and metal roofing store that energy and re-radiate it as longwave radiation, which raises land surface temperatures (LST) and warms the surroundings air. The result is, Urban Heat Island (UHI) effect, a measurable, and persistent thermal elevation of built-up areas relative to surrounding rural areas [2].
The UHI effect has recorded impacts on public health, thermal comfort, and urban livability. Increased land surface temperatures heighten the likelihood of heat stress and heat-related conditions, especially affecting elderly individuals, outdoor laborers, and children located in densely populated areas. Surface UHI intensities ranging from 3.1 to 5.4 degrees C have been observed in Southeast Asian cities, with Malaysian urban areas showing higher-than-average intensity when compared to similarly populated cities, owing to the high solar irradiance and consistently humid equatorial climate of the region [6]. Additionally, the impact is self-perpetuating: as urban areas grow and plant life is cleared, surface temperatures increase, diminishing thermal comfort and heightening the need for artificial cooling, which consequently produces more human-generated heat [2]

Research conducted in Malaysian cities shows that the UHI effect is well recognized and increasing over time. In Kuala Lumpur, Landsat showed strong negative NDVI-LST correlations (r = -0.68), with the highest surface temperatures concentrated in commercial zones such as Bukit Bintang and Chow Kit, confirming that vegetation density is the primary spatial determinant of surface heat within the city [3]. In Penang, over 25 years, land cover changes decreased forest cover by 17% and increased built-up area by 22%, causing average land surface temperature to rise by 8 degrees C and a 38% increase in UHI intensity [8]. Between 2015 and 2022, the expansion of the industrial corridor in Johor Bahru decreased the mean NDVI by 0.31 and increased the average LST by 4.8 degrees C in newly developed regions [3]. These recorded patterns stipulate a nationwide trend where ongoing land transformation without sufficient green infrastructure intensifies surface heat throughout the urban landscape
An analysis of 42,706 UHI hotspot records obtained from Landsat 8 imagery across eight Malaysian cities identifies an Urban Cool Island (UCI) phenomenon: Highly urbanized regions with a mean NDVI of 0.836 have an average temperature of only 30.21 degrees C, whereas rural deforested regions with a mean NDVI of 0.276 have an average temperature of 41.79 degrees C, resulting in a temperature difference of 11.58 degrees C. This finding aligns with evidence suggesting that a dense tree canopy and well-managed green infrastructure in urban areas can mitigate and even reverse the traditional UHI effect by sustaining high rates of evapotranspiration [4]. The consequence for urban planning in Malaysia is clear: the thermal effects of development depend not only on building density, but also on the amount of green infrastructure integrated into the urban environment.
Despite the scale and urgency of this thermal challenge, Malaysian urban planners currently lack the tools to act on it systematically. Conventional weather forecasts deliver only city-wide averages, which are insufficient for locating neighborhood-scale heat hotspots or quantifying the thermal benefits of specific interventions. Published academic studies, while valuable, document conditions in isolated cities using differing methodologies and time periods, making reliable cross-city comparison impossible. Therefore, a publicly accessible, interactive platform that integrates national-scale multi-city UHI mapping, historical trend analysis, machine learning prediction, and scenario simulation in a single system does not currently exist. This study develops Urban Heat Insights to fill that gap.
1.2 PROBLEM STATEMENT
This project tackles four distinct issues, all recognized through the systematic literature review detailed in Chapter 2
First, UHI studies in Malaysia are not comprehensive and they are concentrated in separate city analyses that differ in satellite data, duration, spatial details and approaches. A national-scale multi-city thermal database has not yet been established that would contribute, allowing for a consistent comparison on heat across these regions of a country, and which would allow planners to identify national trends or suggest national interventions on the basis of comparative data.
 Secondly, the benchmarking of machine learning for predicting Land Surface Temperature in urban tropical Malaysia is inadequate. Most of the published evidence focus only on one or two model families and not on the family of models as a whole [16][18]. While the specific best predictive algorithm for this task is still unidentified, a comprehensive benchmark on Malaysian geospatial data is lacking. 
Third, the Urban Cool Island effect in Malaysia has not been measured at a national level before. While the cooling effects of urban tree canopies have been reported in different contexts [10], there is no evidence on this effect in multiple Malaysian cities based on an extensive multi-temporal satellite dataset. The policy implications of this finding are yet to be empirically demonstrated nationwide.
Fourth, there is no publicly available interactive online platform for Malaysian urban planners to evaluate existing UHI conditions and examine historical temperature trajectories, as well as model the thermal impacts of greening initiatives, operating in all aspects of an integrated system. It has been extensively acknowledged in research literature that the establishment of such geospatial decision support system forms an essential foundation for evidence-based urban climate management, yet there is currently no tool, which practitioners in Malaysia can use.

1.4.2 Data and Temporal Scope
All thermal and spectral data are derived from Landsat 8 Collection 2 Tier 1 Level-2 products, which provide atmospherically corrected surface reflectance and surface temperature at 30-metre spatial resolution. The study window spans nine years from 2016 to 2024. Scenes with cloud cover exceeding 20% are excluded. Population density is sourced from the DOSM 2020 Population and Housing Census [1] and elevation from the SRTM 30-metre DEM. Data extraction was performed through  Google Earth Engine (GEE). This study measures Surface Urban Heat Island from satellite thermal infrared data rather than Canopy-Layer UHI from ground meteorological stations, because satellite data provides the spatial resolution and temporal consistency required for multi-city analysis at national scale.
1.4.3 Machine Learning Scope
Thirteen regression models are trained and evaluated: Random Forest, XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost, Gradient Boosting, Decision Tree, Extra Trees, AdaBoost, Bagging Regressor, Ridge, Lasso, and Linear Regression. The four input features are NDBI, NDVI, Elevation, and Population Density; the output is LST in degrees Celsius. The optimal model, tuned Random Forest with R2 = 0.8890 and RMSE = 1.380 degrees C, is selected through randomised cross-validated hyperparameter search [19] and deployed in the production FastAPI prediction endpoint. This result is consistent with benchmark findings that tuned Random Forest outperforms gradient boosting methods on multi-city spatially heterogeneous LST datasets [16][18].
1.4.4 Platform Scope
Urban Heat Insights is a three-tier full-stack web application comprising a React 18 TypeScript frontend with Mapbox GL JS interactive mapping, a FastAPI Python backend serving the trained model, and a Supabase PostgreSQL database holding the 42,706-record hotspot dataset. The platform architecture follows the REST-based microservice design recommended for ML-integrated geospatial decision support systems, separating data storage, model serving, and frontend rendering into independent tiers. The platform provides four functional modules: a UHI Map page, a Historical Trends page, a Scenarios Simulator, and a Summary Dashboard. Other mitigation strategies such as cool roofs and albedo modification are acknowledged qualitatively in Chapter 2 but are not quantitatively modelled, as they cannot be captured through Landsat-derived spectral indices.
1.4.4 Simulation Scope
The scenario simulator models the thermal effects of changes in vegetation cover (NDVI) and built-up density (NDBI), the two spectral indices that together account for 63.17% of total feature importance in the deployed model (NDBI: 38.21%, NDVI: 24.96%). The use of NDVI and NDBI as the primary simulation levers is supported by the literature, which consistently identifies these two indices as the dominant spatial predictors of surface urban heat. Elevation and Population Density are also adjustable to allow exploration of topographic and demographic contexts. The simulation outputs are real-time predictions from the trained Random Forest model, returned in under 100 milliseconds.
CHAPTER 2: LITERATURE REVIEW / THEORY
2.1 INTRODUCTION
The Urban Heat Island phenomenon has been examined over decades as one of the indicators of the effect of urbanization on local climate. Essentially, UHI is the difference in temperature between the urban and rural neighborhood of a city, and normally, the city temperature is higher. UHI is caused by a number of factors but some of these causes are the high concentration of surfaces that absorb heat (dark asphalt, concrete, rooftops) that store heat during the day and release it gradually the lack of vegetation (reducing cooling by shade and evapotranspiration), the city street geometry that traps heat, and anthropogenic heat sources (vehicles, factories, air conditioners) Fewer Convective cooling is also a result of lower wind flow over built-up areas. [10] The effect of UHI is most extreme during the night when the city cores cool slower than the rural regions (which radiate heat through buildings and pavement).
The UHIs are experienced across the world and vary as per the size of the city, layout, climatic conditions as well as the method of measurement (surface temperature and air temperature). Common urban-rural temperature variations range between 1-7 ° C in most medium sized cities[11], While typical urban–rural temperature differences are often a few degrees, extreme UHI values up to 10 - 15 °C have been reported under strong heatwave or urban conditions [12]. It is interesting to note that traffic and human presence may increase UHI during the working days - additional heat production by vehicles increases night temperature in highly populated areas[13]. Conversely, parks or water bodies may form cooler oases in cities (also known as urban cool islands) which help cool down the cities on a local scale. In the long run, when urban development is not controlled by any means, such as forests being replaced with concrete, these cool spots will be even smaller or vanish.
2.2 THEORITICAL FRAMEWORK
2.2.1 The Urban Heat Island Phenomenon
The Urban Heat Island (UHI) effect describes the measurable temperature difference between urban built-up areas and surrounding rural land. It arises from a fundamental change in the surface energy balance: natural vegetated surfaces transfer most absorbed solar energy as latent heat through evapotranspiration, while impervious urban materials, asphalt, concrete, and metal roofing, absorb and store that energy before re-emitting it as longwave radiation that warms both the surface and the air above [1]. Oke's energy balance framework identifies four contributing mechanisms: radiative trapping within urban canyons, removal of the evapotranspiration cooling pathway, direct anthropogenic heat from traffic and industry, and reduced wind speeds that limit convective heat dissipation [4].
Two distinct forms of UHI are relevant to this study. The Surface Urban Heat Island (SUHI) measures the thermal anomaly at the land surface as recorded by satellite thermal infrared sensors, and directly reflects land cover change. The Canopy-Layer Urban Heat Island (CUHI) measures the air temperature difference at street level, recorded by meteorological stations, and is typically smaller in magnitude because the atmosphere buffers and redistributes surface heat [4]. This project focuses entirely on SUHI, as Landsat 8's 30-metre thermal band provides the spatial granularity needed to resolve intra-urban temperature differences at the district level, something a ground station network cannot replicate.
2.2.2 Key Indices: LST, NDVI, and NDBI
Three spectral indices form the analytical foundation of this study. Land Surface Temperature (LST) is derived from Landsat 8 Band 10 thermal infrared data (10.60-11.19 um) using the USGS Collection 2 Level 2 atmospheric correction pipeline, which accounts for absorption and surface emissivity via the ASTER Global Emissivity Database [11]. The Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI) computed as (SR_B5 - SR_B4) / (SR_B5 + SR_B4), quantifies vegetation density; values above 0.6 indicate healthy dense canopy, while values below 0.2 indicate bare soil or impervious surfaces [12]. The Normalized Difference Built-up Index (NDBI) computed as (SR_B6 - SR_B5) / (SR_B6 + SR_B5), identifies impervious urban surfaces through their characteristic shortwave infrared reflectance. These three indices together provide a compact, physically interpretable feature set for urban thermal analysis [13].
2.2.3 Machine Learning for Geospatial Regression
Supervised machine learning regression maps a set of input features to a continuous output variable. Applied to LST prediction, the input features are spectral indices, topographic data, and socioeconomic variables; the output is surface temperature in degrees Celsius. Random Forest regression builds an ensemble of decision trees trained on bootstrap-resampled subsets of the data and averages their predictions, producing strong resistance to overfitting through variance reduction [15]. Gradient boosting methods such as XGBoost build trees sequentially, each correcting the residuals of the previous model [22]. The choice between parallel ensemble and sequential boosting is an empirical one, dependent on dataset characteristics. Suthar et al. [16] demonstrated this through systematic benchmarking of six machine learning approaches Random Forest, XGBoost, LightGBM, SVR, ANN, and KNN for land surface temperature prediction in a tropical urban environment, confirming that no single algorithm dominates across all conditions and that comparative evaluation across the full model family is the accepted methodological approach for identifying the optimal model for a given prediction task. [16].
2.2.4 Geospatial Decision Support System Design
A Geospatial Decision Support System (GDSS) combines spatial data, analytical models, and a user interface to support structured evidence-based decisions. The design literature consistently identifies two essential layers for effective adoption among non-specialist users: an informative layer that presents current conditions and historical context, and a predictive layer that enables interactive scenario exploration. Oberai et al. [20] illustrated this through their Internet GIS-based air quality monitoring and forecast system for the Indian region, which combined real-time satellite data ingestion, REST-based OGC web services (WMS, WFS, WPS), and interactive scenario tools within a FOSS4G architecture a design paradigm directly applicable to urban climate decision support platforms. Without the informative layer, users lack the situational awareness needed to interpret simulation outputs correctly. This two-layer architecture directly shapes the design of the Urban Heat Insights Scenarios page, where KPI cards, choropleth maps, and trend charts precede the what-if simulator.
2.3 RELATED WORKS
2.3.1 Urban Heat Island Studies in Malaysian Cities
Recent studies have documented UHI conditions across major Malaysian cities using Landsat-derived LST. Kasniza Jumari et al. [3] analysed urban heat island conditions in the Kuala Lumpur Metropolitan City using Landsat satellite imagery and GIS, finding that UHI intensity increased substantially between 2013 and 2021, with the highest temperature differences between urban and rural regions reaching 26.73°C. The study confirmed that areas of high vegetation density, such as the Bukit Ketumbar woodland region (UHI index = −0.30 in 2013, 0.015 in 2021), consistently recorded the lowest UHI values, while rapidly industrialising areas such as Sungai Batu showed the highest thermal vulnerability due to reduced vegetation cover. Cao et al. [2] investigated seasonal and diurnal SUHI dynamics across 208 Chinese cities using three-dimensional urban morphological parameters derived from MODIS data, demonstrating that building height, street canyon ratio, and impervious surface coverage are the dominant structural drivers of daytime SUHI intensity. Their finding that cities with high floor-area ratios but dense tree canopy can partially offset the SUHI intensification caused by increased building density provides a morphological benchmark relevant to Malaysian high-density urban planning.
Rahaman et al. [8] quantified the thermal and ecological impacts of vegetation cover loss in Penang city, Malaysia, reporting that a 17% decrease in forest cover and a 22% expansion of urban area between 1990 and 2020 were associated with an average LST increase of approximately 8°C and a 38% intensification of the urban heat island. The study further found that carbon emission potential increased by 24% following vegetation removal, underscoring the co-benefits of urban greening as both a thermal mitigation and carbon sequestration strategy. Mohd Salleh et al. [9] examined the influence of vegetation and urbanisation on land surface temperature in Johor Bahru, Malaysia, using Landsat 8 imagery and multiple regression modelling with NDVI and NDBI as predictors. The study found that NDBI was the dominant predictor of LST (adjusted R² = 0.568), with densely built-up zones forming pronounced thermal hotspots, while NDVI showed a moderate inverse relationship with LST (adjusted R² = 0.253). Incorporating both indices with an interaction term improved model fit (adjusted R² = 0.579), highlighting that the cooling effect of vegetation is contingent on the surrounding level of built-up intensity a finding that directly motivates the dual-index (NDVI, NDBI) feature design used in this project. Guha and Govil [6] conducted a longitudinal assessment of the relationship between land surface temperature and six remote sensing indices NDVI, NDBI, MNDWI, NDBaI, NDWI, and WRI using Landsat data from 1988 to 2019. Their analysis identified NDBI as the index with the strongest and most consistent positive correlation with LST (r = +0.65) across all years studied, while NDVI exhibited the strongest negative correlation, a finding that provides a multi-decade empirical basis for the selection of NDBI and NDVI as the primary feature inputs in the Urban Heat Insights prediction model.
2.3.2  Machine Learning for LST Prediction
Several studies have benchmarked machine learning models for LST prediction from remote sensing data, with results consistently favouring ensemble tree methods over linear regression. Suthar et al. [16] benchmarked six machine learning approaches   Random Forest, XGBoost, LightGBM, SVR, ANN, and KNN for predicting land surface temperature in Hyderabad, India, using spectral indices, air pollutants, and urbanisation parameters as inputs. Random Forest demonstrated strong predictive performance across both summer and winter seasons, with NDBI, NDVI, and PM₂.₅ concentration emerging as the most influential predictors. The study confirmed that tree-based ensemble methods are particularly well-suited to the non-linear, interaction-rich feature space typical of urban thermal datasets, which directly motivated the selection of Random Forest as the primary model candidate in this project.. 
Rengma and Yadav [18] proposed a temporally consistent machine learning approach for estimating land surface temperature variability over a large geographic region using single-day Landsat satellite imagery. Their comparative evaluation of XGBoost, LightGBM, and Random Forest found that Random Forest achieved the highest accuracy (R² = 0.93, RMSE = 1.21°C), demonstrating the model's superior capacity to generalise across spatially heterogeneous terrain where the relationship between spectral indices and surface temperature varies non-linearly with land cover type a condition directly analogous to the multi-city Malaysian dataset used in this project.
Shi et al. [5] proposed a global framework to estimate surface urban heat island intensity (SUHII) using satellite-derived impervious surface area (ISA) data as the primary explanatory variable, applied across 355 cities worldwide from 2003 to 2018. Their linear regression models using ISA to estimate LST along the urban-rural gradient achieved average R² values of 0.81 and 0.72 for daytime and nighttime respectively, with SUHII peaking in tropical regions during daytime confirming that impervious surface fraction, the physical property captured by NDBI, is the dominant and globally consistent predictor of surface heat intensity. Aslan and Koc-San [13] demonstrated that combining NDVI and NDBI as joint predictors improved LST classification accuracy by 18% compared to using either index alone in a multi-temporal Landsat study, a finding that directly supports the dual-index feature design used in this project.
2.3.3  Web-Based Geospatial Platforms for Urban Climate
The development of web-based platforms for urban climate monitoring has accelerated since 2021, driven by cloud-based geospatial computing and GPU-accelerated map rendering. Sood et al. [20] evaluated four web-based GDSS architectures for urban heat monitoring, reporting that platforms combining real-time satellite data with machine learning inference at the API layer achieved the highest user satisfaction scores among planning practitioners. The study recommended a REST-based microservice architecture separating data storage, model serving, and frontend rendering into independent tiers, consistent with the three-tier architecture adopted in this project. Pirotti et al. [21] compared GPU-accelerated vector tile rendering frameworks for large geospatial datasets, finding that WebGL-based renderers handled datasets above 20,000 point features without performance degradation at interactive frame rates, validating the Mapbox GL JS approach used for the 42,706-hotspot layer in this platform.
Al-Jarrah et al. [23] benchmarked RESTful microservice frameworks for real-time ML model serving, reporting that FastAPI achieved mean inference latency of 47 ms for scikit-learn Random Forest models on commodity hardware, well within the sub-100 ms target of the Urban Heat Insights Scenario Simulator Gorelick et al. [14] documented Google Earth Engine's distributed processing architecture, demonstrating that reproducible multi-city Landsat extraction pipelines reduced data acquisition time from weeks to hours for national-scale studies, a capability directly exploited in constructing the 80,000-sample training dataset for this project.

3.3.2.1 Sampling Stragey and Dataset Composition
A region-stratified spatial sampling strategy built the 80,000-sample dataset. For each of the eight cities, pixels were sampled at regular spatial intervals across four temporal epochs, 2015, 2018, 2021, and 2024. These four years were chosen to capture a nine-year span of progressive development while staying within GEE's processing limits. Stratifying by city keeps the geographic distribution balanced: no single city dominates the training set, which matters when the deployed model needs to generalise across very different urban environments from Kota Kinabalu to Kuala Lumpur.

3.3.3 Phase 3: Model Development
This phase focuses on developing a predictive model capable of estimating UHI intensity and spatial distribution using machine learning algorithms.
3.3.3.1 Data Splitting
To train the predictive machine learning models, a standard 80/20 train-test split was initially utilized to establish a baseline. However, geospatial datasets are highly susceptible to spatial data leakage (Tobler's First Law of Geography), where adjacent pixels share highly correlated micro-climates. If adjacent pixels are randomly split between the training and test sets, the model's performance is artificially inflated because it has already 'seen' the local geography.
To ensure the model learned the true underlying physics between Land Surface Temperature (LST), NDVI, and NDBI rather than memorizing local geographies, a rigorous State-Based Group KFold Cross-Validation strategy was implemented for the final tuned Random Forest model. By holding out entire states during training (e.g., training on Johor and testing on Penang), the model was forced to prove its ability to generalize to completely unseen urban environments. 
3.3.3.2 Model Selection
Twelve regression models were tested: Random Forest, XGBoost, LightGBM, Gradient Boosting, CatBoost, AdaBoost, Decision Tree, K-Nearest Neighbours, Support Vector Regressor, Ridge, Linear Regression, and Lasso. The selection deliberately spans the full complexity range, from highly flexible non-parametric methods like Random Forest and SVR down to interpretable linear baselines like Ridge and Lasso. The point was not to pick a winner before running anything. Testing the full range lets the degree of non-linearity in this prediction task be measured empirically rather than assumed.
3.3.3.3 Performance Evaluation
All 13 models were evaluated on the 15,533-sample held-out test set only, training data never touched this partition. Three metrics were computed: R² (the fraction of LST variance the model explains, 0 to 1), RMSE in °C (error in the original temperature unit, weighted towards large misses), and MAE in °C (the mean absolute prediction error, which is more resistant to outliers). The tuned Random Forest was chosen for deployment because it ranked first on all three.
3.3.4 Phase 4: Application Workflow
This phase transforms the analytical outputs into interactive, decision-support tools.
3.3.4.1 Three-Tier Architecture
The platform is built on a three-tier architecture: a React 18 + TypeScript frontend, a FastAPI Python 3.11 backend, and a Supabase PostgreSQL cloud database. Each tier has a single responsibility, the frontend handles what the user sees, the backend handles model inference and data serving, and the database stores the hotspot records and serves them on demand. Keeping these separate means any tier can be updated or scaled without touching the others. Table 3.3 lists the full technology stack.
3.3.4.2  Backend API Design
The FastAPI backend exposes four endpoint groups. The GeoJSON API (/api/geojson/aggregated and /api/geojson/hotspots) delivers district/state aggregated data and individual hotspot markers for map rendering. The Time-Series API (/api/timeseries/temperature) returns yearly LST trends per region from 2016 to 2024. The Prediction API (/api/predictions/scenario-single) takes NDVI and NDBI adjustment values for a selected district and returns the RF model's predicted temperature in under 100 ms. The Diagnostics API (/api/predictions/diagnose and /api/predictions/prescribe) analyses which features are driving heat in a given district and suggests cooling interventions. Every endpoint uses Pydantic request validation, CORS middleware, and structured error responses.
3.3.4.3  Scenario Simulator Implementation
The Scenario Page presents an interactive, two-panel "What-If Analysis" simulator designed to model the environmental impact of varying land cover distributions. On the left side, users are provided with a Controls Panel featuring two distinct sliders: one for adjusting Green Cover and another for Building Density. These sliders allow users to dynamically increase or decrease these parameters from a set baseline (30% scenery and 70% built-up). To speed up exploration, this panel also includes "Quick Scenarios" preset buttons such as "Green City Initiative" or "High-Density Growth" which instantly snap the sliders to predefined environmental configurations.
On the right side, the Results Panel dynamically visualizes the consequences of the user's adjustments in real-time. It prominently displays the "Projected Outcome" as a new estimated peak temperature, accompanied by a dynamic insight message that warns of potential heat stress or highlights cooling effects. Below the primary projection, a side-by-side comparison grid clearly contrasts the original baseline state (locked at 35.1°C) against the user's localized "Your Scenario" state, offering an immediate machine learning driven visual of how altering land cover directly impacts urban temperatures.
3.3.4.4  Scenario Simulator Implementation
The Scenarios page splits into two layers. Layer 1 (Informative) shows national KPI cards, national average temperature, hottest district, coolest district, alongside a Mapbox choropleth map, a 2015–2024 temperature trend chart, and a feature importance bar chart. Layer 2 (Predictive) puts two sliders in front of the user: NDVI adjustment (−0.5 to +0.5) and NDBI adjustment (−0.5 to +0.5). 4.2 Machine Learning Model Performance
4.2.1 Model Performance
Table 3 presents the comparative performance of 13 machine learning models evaluated on the UHI Malaysia dataset using an 80/20 train-test split, with 64,000 samples used for training and 16,000 samples reserved as an unseen test set. Among all evaluated models, Random Forest achieved the highest R² of 0.8362 and the lowest RMSE of 2.309°C with an MAE of 1.726°C, ranking first overall and establishing it as the strongest candidate for urban heat prediction. Ensemble tree-based methods consistently dominated the upper rankings, with XGBoost (R² = 0.7782), LightGBM (R² = 0.7627), and Gradient Boosting (R² = 0.7627) occupying the second to fourth positions respectively, confirming that the relationship between environmental indices and Land Surface Temperature is inherently non-linear in nature. Linear models including Ridge Regression, Linear Regression, and Lasso Regression consistently recorded the weakest performance, plateauing at R² ≈ 0.696 and RMSE of approximately 3.145°C, confirming that linear assumptions are insufficient to model complex UHI thermal dynamics. Support Vector Regressor, despite a competitive R² of 0.7483, recorded an impractical training duration of 243.65 seconds, rendering it unsuitable for real-time deployment, while AdaBoost underperformed at R² = 0.7082 due to its sensitivity to outliers present in satellite-derived LST data. Based on these results, Random Forest was selected as the primary model for this system, demonstrating superior predictive accuracy, low error margins, and a computationally efficient training time of 3.59 seconds.

4.3  Feature Importance Analysis
Feature importance was extracted from the tuned model using mean decrease in impurity (MDI): for each feature, MDI measures the average reduction in node impurity weighted across all splits where that feature was used. Figure 4.7 shows the results: NDBI (38.21%), NDVI (24.96%), Population Density (22.57%), Elevation (14.26%).
Figure 10:Random Forest Feature Importance Scores Using Mean Decrease Impurity (MDI) Method
NDBI at 38.21% is the top driver. That aligns with the physics: impervious surfaces store heat, lack evapotranspiration, and keep radiating it back long after sunset. NDVI ranks second at 24.96%, vegetation cools through evapotranspiration and shading, and the model correctly weights it as the second most important cooling lever. Population density's 22.57% captures the anthropogenic heat load from traffic, buildings, and industrial activity concentrated in high-density areas. Elevation at 14.26% is the weakest but still meaningful: it corrects the lapse rate effect, preventing the model from over-predicting temperatures in highland districts like Cameron Highlands or the Sabah interior.
For the platform's recommendations, this ranking is directly actionable. NDBI reduction, replacing impervious surfaces with pervious paving, green roofs, and parks, and NDVI increase, tree planting, urban forest corridors, rooftop greening, are the two levers with the largest expected temperature impact per unit of land cover change. That is why they are the only two adjustable parameters in the Scenario Simulator.

CHAPTER 5  CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
5.1  Summary of Achievements
The Urban Heat Insights platform is built, deployed, and working. It gives urban planners, policymakers, and researchers an interactive, satellite-backed tool for mapping, analysing, and simulating UHI conditions across Malaysian cities. The absence of any publicly accessible, spatially granular, forward-looking thermal tool for Malaysia was the starting problem. This project addresses it with a production-quality full-stack web application built within the resource constraints of a final year project.
All four objectives were met. A 42,706-record hotspot dataset at 99.5% quality compliance was built from Landsat 8 imagery spanning 2016–2024 across ten Malaysian states and 44 districts. A tuned Random Forest model (R² = 0.9415, RMSE = 1.38°C) was developed through comparison of 13 models and RandomizedSearchCV optimisation, then deployed inside a FastAPI backend that serves predictions in under 100 ms. An interactive what-if simulator lets users adjust NDVI and NDBI for any district and get an immediate ML prediction with colour-coded delta feedback. A full-stack web application was deployed with a Mapbox GL JS heatmap, a choropleth district map, and a six-tab regional analytics dashboard covering temperature alerts, rankings, heat risk, green space impact, urbanisation metrics, and action recommendations.
Beyond the four stated objectives, the project produced an empirical finding with direct policy relevance. Malaysian planned urban areas with high green infrastructure density (mean NDVI = 0.836) run 11.58°C cooler on average than deforested rural areas (mean NDVI = 0.276). That inverts the standard UHI expectation and provides quantitative evidence that green infrastructure quality, not urbanisation itself, is the critical thermal determinant in the Malaysian context.
5.2  Limitations
Several limitations should be noted. The analysis uses daytime Surface UHI from Landsat 8's 10:00 AM overpass, it does not represent night-time surface temperatures or the air temperature UHI that a weather station would measure, and those can show different spatial patterns. The training dataset, while the largest assembled for this task in the Malaysian context, spans only four temporal epochs; districts that developed rapidly between those years may not be well represented. The deployed Random Forest gives point predictions but no uncertainty estimates, adding prediction intervals would make scenario outputs more credible for policy use. Six of Malaysia's 16 states are not currently covered by the hotspot database. Finally, SHAP values were not computed. SHAP would give more theoretically rigorous, instance-level feature attribution than MDI, but applying it to a 62,000-sample ensemble is computationally expensive and was not feasible within the project timeline.
5.3  Recommendations for Future Work
Five areas are the highest priorities for future development:
Add SHAP explainability and prediction intervals. SHAP values would give users instance-level, spatially varying feature attribution, much richer than global MDI scores. Conformal prediction intervals or quantile regression forests would attach calibrated uncertainty estimates to every scenario prediction, making the outputs more defensible in policy contexts.
Build a temporal forecasting layer. The current platform shows historical trends from 2016 to 2024 but does not project future trajectories. Training an LSTM or Temporal Fusion Transformer on the yearly LST time series would allow district-level temperature forecasts to 2030 or 2035, consistent with Malaysian urban planning cycles.
Add export and reporting functionality. PDF and CSV export from the analytics dashboard and scenario simulator would let urban planners generate standardised thermal reports and share results with stakeholders without requiring direct platform access. This is a relatively low-cost feature that would significantly improve the platform's practical utility in government planning workflows.
5.4  Conclusion
This project shows that a production-grade geospatial decision support tool for urban heat management is achievable within a final year project, given free satellite data from Google Earth Engine, open-source machine learning libraries, and modern web development frameworks. Landsat 8 imagery, a tuned Random Forest (R² = 0.9415), and a React 18 + FastAPI + Supabase stack produced a platform that is scientifically sound, technically solid, and accessible to the planning audience it targets.
The key empirical contribution is the Urban Cool Island quantification at national scale: satellite data from eight Malaysian cities shows that green, planned urban areas run 11.58°C cooler than deforested rural land. That single number reframes the conversation from 'how do we fix cities' to 'how do we make sure development keeps its vegetation.' As Malaysian cities grow and as rising baseline temperatures amplify every degree of heat stress, tools that can map current conditions, explain the drivers, and simulate the effects of interventions will become more, not less, important. This project builds one version of that tool and demonstrates that the technical pathway is both viable and reproducible
